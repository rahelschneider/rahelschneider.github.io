Evaluation of image recognition systems

This page showcases the development of a remote tourism experience by me, Rahel Schneider, and my team members Hideki Kama, Noriko Asada, and Keita Shigeno. We followed the Double Diamond design thinking process to build a prototype of a remote tourist experience.

The starting point of our user research was marked by receiving Cultural Probes. In our case, every team member prepared the Cultural Probes on their own before the first meeting. As we designed for our own needs and the Cultural Probes were provided by a third party, the method is thoroughly reasonable for gathering user data.

To structure and analyze the collected data we used Affinity Diagramming.

To get an even deeper understanding of the user we tried to create an Empathy Map.

After finishing the analysis of our user data, we formulated an actionable problem statement:

“Taro, a young man who lives in Japan and has never been abroad...needs a way to get in touch with people in other countries and learn about their cultures...because...he grew up on the island of Japan and wants to learn about other countries and cultures and make friends all over the world so he can visit them when he has enough money.”

To create ideas that could solve our problem we used three different methods.

Before we designed the actual prototype, we created a wireframe and answered some open questions.

For our prototype, we decided to prepare static images with overlayed GUI controls. As we had german and japanese students in our group, we created one German experience and one Japanese experience.

We tested our prototype with 3 participants. We conducted the prototype tests via Zoom and followed the Wizard of Oz principle.

During the prototype tests, it turned out that the users wanted to be able to enter buildings. This is the reason for the additional screens in our final prototype. Users who enabled direct translation sometimes started to speak English again, without disabling direct translation. We suspect that this was due to a lack of visibility of system status. Consequently, we added a central banner in the upper half of each frame that indicates currently translated languages. Additionally, we observed that users do not see and understand all provided controls. Our solution to this problem is a short introduction of all controls at the start of each VR trip. The last alteration we made to the prototype was adding more people to choose from. Mainly because users were confused by not having a choice. For the remaining questions, we got validating answers from our user tests. Additionally, there were no signs of the need for scripted conversations and most importantly, all users wanted to be friends with the person they visited.
